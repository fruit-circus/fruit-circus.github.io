Here’s an object:
a person-shaped machine.


A device so ancient
that it can hardly be considered the same object that it was when it was created.
When its parts break or warp, it adds new ones,
and over eons it becomes more and more alive.


Only a child, sent to live in the long-nighted world,
where cherry trees bloom at the same time each year,
like cogs that catch a regular notch in the brass.
A peaceful world, with a smooth iron heart under the magma.
She develops an implicit intuition of the eternal sway of things,
an unavoidable consequence of living on a geologic time scale:
the sense of when a solar flare is coming, when the ice is leaving,
or when a magnetic shift is due.
.
.


Your memories all come flooding back in a wave.

It begins with the aftermath of the Carrington event, busted telegraph wires and small fires in the streets. You had innate knowledge of what a telegraph wire was, somehow, but you had no idea who you were. People recognized you on the street, asking if you were alright, but you hurried past them, confused and frightened. Following that, gaps began to be filled, but uncovering the evidence of your old self and their life their friends and their interests and concerns only made you feel further from who you had become. And then, for another hundred years, you remade yourself. You tried to remember.

Looking at the ceiling stars, you get the vague impression of yourself as a child wandering around in the Archean era, trying to skip stones on the lava. Teetering about, staring at the sky and wondering if there was anyone else out there. But you can’t remember it clearly, and even then, you may as well have been a different person. Even the person you are now, according to your newly restored memory, seems distant. It was only yesterday that you got back from the hike with your cousins, but it seems like it was a billion years ago too.

It took you an embarrassingly long time to figure out that solar flares were responsible for your memory loss, and even longer to engineer the genius solution of putting a backup data chip in a tin-plated steel box. But hey, there’s no one else like you in the whole world, and there hasn’t been for hundreds of millions of years. Well — you don’t actually remember that far back, but you get the feeling that you’ve been working things out on your own for a long time.

But even then, without the specifics, without knowing the names of the people that you decided to become after the old ones were erased, you still remember the smell of iron, the grime, the ocean salt, the pollen, the wisteria, the wind chimes. Those things are seated much deeper in your body than the bright dot in your forehead called a name.

The magnetic poles shift as the outer core swirls, but the inner core is always there, hiding. The inner self that never forgets its original purpose. To have fun. To explore. To help. To love a lot. To wake up in a room and hear a bluejay, and the wind rustling a thousand aimless patterns on the trees.
