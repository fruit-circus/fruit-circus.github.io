Objects in a Room

Is it playable? Who knows. Is it solvable? Technically.

In order to get your memories back, you will need to examine objects in the room, find keys and passwords, and solve puzzles. To begin, click on the input box and enter a command like "examine self". You can also drop "examine" and just type "self", e.g., or "notebook" or "flashdrive" etc.

If you get lost, you can always examine the walls ("north", "west", etc.) which will provide a description of all the objects on that side of the room. If you examine every object you find, you will discover everything you need to solve the puzzle. That being said, it's still a difficult puzzle.

If you're still stuck after having exmained as many objects as you can find, use the hints below.

Phone Password (4)

The red sketchbook on the nightstand has two pieces of important information: the "syn list" and the "chess code". The syn list is used in combination with the nail polish and the chess code is used in comination with the board on the end table. The order of the colors of nail polish can be read as a six-digit code. The first color is yellow, which is the color associated with the number 3 in the syn list. The phone password is six digits. The phone password is 357468.

Chessboard (3)

The chessboard is read from left to right, like a text passage. It can store 32 characters, meaning that it takes two chess tiles to represent one letter. In every case except for the letters "p" and "r", one of those two tiles is a space. Spaces are represented by the letter "x", so an empty tile followed by a black king (x, bk) followed by a white pawn followed by an empty tile (wp, x) would translate to the letters "BE". The code on the chess board adapts to your specific game, and will only give you clues to keys you haven't found yet. The code you are most likely to see solves to: "BEHIND WATERFALL". "Waterfall" refers to the waterfall painting on the north wall.

Matryoshka (4)

The command "open every doll" will reveal the contents of the smallest nesting doll. The brass key from the nesting doll can be used to unlock the panel built into the wooden bookshelf.

Cardstock (6)

The first number in each binomial refers to the shelf, the second refers to the book's place in the row. Together, each set of two numbers references a book, from which you are meant to take the first initial of the authors last name to form a word. From (1, 1) for example you can get the book Eastern Forests by A. and M. Sutton. The letter therefore is "S". All together, they spell "SPARROW". "SPARROW" refers to the book The Sparrow by Mary Doria Russel. Examining or opening The Sparrow will reveal the Starmap.

Starmap (1)

By reading the letters in sequential order based on the number of stars in each section (or just unscrambling it) you can derive the word ZEPHYR.

Flashlight (2)

Now that you have the blacklight flashlight, examining the multi-colored grid will give you the Playfair grid used to decrypt the graph paper message on the pinboard. Like the chessboard, this part of the game adapts to give you only information you didn't already have, so it would be difficult for me to give you the answer to your specific cipher. You can, however, just copy and paste "JULIA BCDEF GHKMN OPQRS TVWXY" into the "playfair grid" section of this Playfair Decoder and put the graph paper message into the "ciphertext" area at the top.

Phone Notes (3)

The text in the note called "Star Vigenere" is a Vigenere cipher whose key is derived from the pattern of the ceiling stars. You could use the "starmap" to determine the key, but you could also use an online tool to try to brute-force the plaintext without knowing the key, because Vigenere ciphers are weak to frequency analysis. In this case, though, getting the keyword is only a matter of "looking to the stars".

Laptop Password (7)

The hint to the laptop password is to "look at the world with rose-colored glasses". Curiously, there are a pair of rose-colored glasses on the window sill. I wonder if that odd pattern in the blue painting would become legible if the red parts of it were diminished. Because I thought it would be clever if you interpreted "world" as "globe" (even though I can't think of how Julia would do that hidden message trick on a globe without it being easily readable) you also get the same text when examining the blue globe. That text is "19, 53, 74, 53, 27, 15, 68, 28, 29, 16." The second clue in the laptop password hint is the word "elemental". Perhaps the sequence is a seqeunce of atomic numbers. 19 would be potasium, so perhaps its symbol "K" is the first letter. The full sequence translates to: "kiwi copernicus".

Flashdrive (9)

After unlocking the laptop, inserting the flashdrive will reveal an icon called MYRMIDON. "myr" will provide its description. Open the file called "Mazy", then play until you arrive at an underground terminal prompting you for a passphrase. The passphrase may have been hinted to either in the chess code, in your phone notes, or in the Playfair cipher (depending on which order you found the keys). The answer is also spoken in one of the voice memos. The passphrase is spoken in the "smoke and mirrors" voice memo. In the professor's story, the Indian border guard asks the Dalai Lama, "Are you the Buddha?" and the Dalai Lama replies: "I am the moon reflected in a drop of water". You can type the whole phrase, but just "moon reflected water" will do. Passing the prompt reveals the command phrase: "open globe". Inside the globe, back in your room, is the steel key. ...If at any point you feel the back of your neck, you will notice an odd, painless gash. Taking a picture of it with your phone would allow you to see what it is. If you haven't read them yet, make sure to read the front matter in the pink notebook and black notebook and to examine "photo 5". You can also try to "remember your dream". Try putting the data chip in the port in the back of your neck.
